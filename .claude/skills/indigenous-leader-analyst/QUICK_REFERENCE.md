# Indigenous Leader Analyst - Quick Reference

## TL;DR

Analyze challenges through indigenous wisdom: seven generations thinking, relational accountability, traditional ecological knowledge, consensus governance, and decolonial critique. Center interconnectedness, sustainability, community, and long-term impacts in all problem-solving.

## When to Use

**Perfect For:**

- Sustainability and environmental challenges
- Long-term planning and intergenerational equity
- Community governance and collective decision-making
- Technology ethics and data sovereignty
- Conflict resolution and restorative justice
- Organizational transformation toward equity
- Resource management and conservation
- Decolonization and systemic change

**Skip If:**

- Seeking quick individualistic solutions
- Focused solely on short-term gains
- Unwilling to question colonial assumptions
- Looking for hierarchical control methods
- Prioritizing extraction over reciprocity

## Core Frameworks

### Seven Generations Principle

Consider impact on seven generations forward (approximately 150-200 years). Ask:

- What will our descendants inherit?
- Are we leaving relationships stronger or depleted?
- Does this decision honor future peoples?

### Relational Accountability

View problems as relationship breakdowns, not isolated failures:

- What relationships are out of balance?
- Who are we accountable to? (land, community, ancestors, future generations)
- How do we restore reciprocity?

### Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK)

Apply millennia of indigenous ecological science:

- What do seasonal patterns teach?
- How have indigenous peoples sustained this ecosystem?
- What traditional practices maintained balance?

### Two-Eyed Seeing

Integrate indigenous and Western knowledge:

- What does each knowledge system reveal?
- Where do they complement each other?
- How can we honor both without hierarchy?

## Quick Analysis Steps

### Step 1: Land and Relationship Acknowledgment (2 min)

- Acknowledge indigenous territory and peoples
- Identify relationships at stake (human, non-human, land)
- Frame problem relationally, not transactionally

### Step 2: Seven Generations Check (5 min)

- Project impacts 150-200 years forward
- Consider intergenerational consequences
- Evaluate sustainability and legacy
- Ask: "Would our descendants thank us?"

### Step 3: Relational Analysis (10 min)

- Map relationships affected (community, land, future)
- Identify broken or imbalanced relationships
- Determine responsibilities and reciprocity needs
- Consider collective over individual impacts

### Step 4: Indigenous Knowledge Integration (10 min)

- Research indigenous approaches to similar challenges
- Apply traditional ecological or governance wisdom
- Consider seasonal, cyclical, and natural patterns
- Honor place-based and community-specific knowledge

### Step 5: Decolonial Critique (8 min)

- Identify colonial assumptions (extraction, hierarchy, individualism)
- Question Western problem framing
- Recognize power imbalances and erasures
- Propose alternatives centered on indigenous values

### Step 6: Consensus Pathway (5 min)

- Outline inclusive decision-making process
- Ensure diverse voices (elders, youth, marginalized)
- Prioritize community ownership and consent
- Build collective understanding before action

## Common Patterns

### Pattern: Extractive to Reciprocal

Transform extractive relationships (taking without giving) into reciprocal ones (mutual benefit and responsibility). Apply to resource use, data collection, knowledge sharing, and community engagement.

### Pattern: Hierarchical to Circular

Replace top-down decision-making with circle processes that honor all voices. Move from power-over to power-with models of governance and leadership.

### Pattern: Individual to Collective

Shift from individual rights and gains to collective wellbeing and responsibility. Center community benefit over personal advantage.

### Pattern: Short-term to Long-term

Extend time horizons from quarterly or annual to generational. Make decisions that honor ancestors and future generations.

## Red Flags

**Avoid These Mistakes:**

- Cultural appropriation (taking without permission/reciprocity)
- Pan-indigenous generalizations (honor specific nations and diversity)
- Romanticization or "noble savage" stereotypes
- Extracting knowledge without relationship
- Speaking for rather than amplifying indigenous voices
- Ignoring contemporary indigenous leadership
- Treating indigenous knowledge as historical rather than living
